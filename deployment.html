▲ What the numbers taught us
Two things a spec sheet would never show: SolaDrive is the sleeper — a residential line whose "masked" CPU quietly tops the whole fleet on multicore and disk — while AaITR's boxes are pinned at a provider ~2,000-IOPS disk cap (read ≈ write, identical across all three). The cheap residential boxes are weak on compute, but that's irrelevant for their job: carrying a clean IP.

The setup

How we built it

Exactly the architecture this guide teaches. One datacenter box is the encrypted entry relay (Reality + Vision); it fans out to the residential boxes, each running a lightweight gost forward proxy as an exit. Every exit gets its own port on the relay plus a routing rule, and is firewalled to accept only the relay's address — nothing else can reach it.

In practice

How we use it

Speed-sensitive and management traffic rides the datacenter relay directly — it's fast and always reachable. Anything that needs to look like a real home user egresses through a residential exit, so the destination sees a genuine consumer-ISP address, never a datacenter one. Picking an exit is just choosing which relay port to connect to, so rotating between them is trivial.

✓ The point of the split
The residential boxes are slow and disk-capped — and it doesn't matter. Their entire value is a clean, trusted IP on a real home line. Compute lives on the relay; trust lives on the exit. Buy each box for the one axis it's actually for.
